# Lane L4 — Business Model & Moderation Operations (Agent Marketplace)

Status: COMPLETE · 2026-07-06 (horizon: 2025–2026, as-of July 2026)
Builds on: `agent-marketplace-foundational-synthesis-2026-07.md` (L1–L3). Does not re-derive the foundational finding — install-time gating alone is insufficient; **the most-exploited failure mode is a trusted publisher pushing a malicious UPDATE to an already-installed package.** This lane stress-tests that finding against how four leaders actually staff and run moderation, and treats monetization as a light secondary pass per the brief's priority order.

Scope note: Sonik's near-term surface is the AGENT marketplace (installable capabilities for an embedded booking-platform agent). The two-sided COMMERCE marketplace is explicitly later; commerce mechanics (escrow, two-sided liquidity) are out of scope here and not researched.

Corroboration convention carried through from L1–L3: **[2+]** = confirmed by 2+ independent sources; **[1]** = single source, flagged; absence-of-evidence gaps are called out explicitly rather than asserted as confirmed negatives.

## 1. Moderation / review operations by player

**Microsoft (Teams Store / AppSource / Copilot agents / Agent 365).** Review is a hybrid of ~400 automated + manual test cases (functionality, UX, Graph permission-scope checks, anti-spam) [1, official], with a stated 24-working-hour test report and ~1-business-day publish for passing submissions [2+]. Agent-specific validation is a distinct, heavier layer under Commercial Marketplace policy 1140.9: mandatory user-disclosure UX for "consequential" actions, explicit prompt-injection safeguard requirements, a 99.9% uptime bar, and ≤9s response-time SLA for 99% of calls [1, but binds to a named legal policy doc]. Newest and most relevant: the **Agent Registry** in the M365 admin center adds an org-admin pre-publish gate — each org's own admins review capabilities/data access/permissions and approve or reject an agent before their users can install it, layered on top of Microsoft's own store review [2+, Learn docs + Security blog, May 2026].

**Salesforce (AppExchange / AgentExchange).** Combines automated scanning (Salesforce Code Analyzer, Checkmarx via Partner Security Portal) with a human "black-box, time-limited" security-review team [1, official, evergreen doc]. Official turnaround is stated as "up to 4 weeks" from a complete submission [1, official]; trade-press estimates (unverified against a fetchable primary fee page) put full review at 6–9 weeks with a $999/submission fee for paid apps, $0 for free [2+, but both secondary]. Agentforce agent actions submitted to AgentExchange are represented as riding the *same* ISVforce security-review pipeline as any other package — no separate agent-specific review pipeline was confirmed in a directly fetchable primary source (the developer.salesforce.com page describing this returned 403; only paraphrased in secondary blogs) [1, flagged for primary confirmation]. AgentExchange itself launched March 2025 as a curated layer atop AppExchange, then was folded into a full AppExchange+Slack+Agentforce rebrand at TDX 2026 (April 2026), retiring the 20-year "AppExchange" name — sources describing it pre- vs. post-April-2026 describe different scopes of the same brand [2+].

**OpenAI (GPT Store / ChatGPT Apps SDK).** GPT Store (2024, stale baseline) used automated content screening → paid third-party vendor review → human reviewers [1, official, pre-2025]. The newer Apps SDK (opened to third-party public submissions Dec 17, 2025) is explicitly weaker on process commitment: OpenAI states it "may perform automated scans or manual reviews" — an either/or, not a guaranteed human pass [1, official] — corroborated directionally by TechCrunch's framing that OpenAI "will be reviewing all new third-party app submissions" without specifying mechanics [2, same underlying claim]. No turnaround SLA is published for either surface, and developers are explicitly told not to request expedited review [1, official]. This is the weakest-staffed review operation of the four players researched, and the newest (still described in its own docs as scaling up).

**Apple / Google (reference precedent, not agent marketplaces).** Apple reviewed 9.1M+ submissions in 2025 (automated + human, AI-assisted "app similarity"/update-diff flagging), rejecting 2M+ (1.2M new, ~800K pending updates) [2+, Apple Newsroom + MacRumors, May 2026]. Google Play review generally takes hours to 7 days, with a notable carve-out: **Internal test track uploads publish immediately and are only "subject to retroactive review after going live"** — an explicit asynchronous/post-hoc review model for one distribution channel [1, official]. Both operate at a scale and staffing depth (Apple terminated 193,000 developer accounts in 2025 for fraud; Google banned 80,000) [1 each, official] that no agent-marketplace player currently matches — useful as an upper-bound reference, not a template Sonik should try to replicate at current scale.

**Cross-player read:** Microsoft is the most mature on agent-specific review depth and is actively building org-admin-level gating (Agent Registry) on top of platform review — this is the single most directly transferable model for Sonik, which also has one org (the booking platform tenant) as the practical unit of trust rather than millions of consumer end users. Salesforce is process-mature but not signing/attestation-hardened. OpenAI is newest and least staffed — a cautionary example of "ship the marketplace before the review muscle," worth naming explicitly since Sonik is at an earlier stage than all three and could repeat this mistake by default.

If Sonik builds a trust-tier system, publishing clear, falsifiable revocation criteria up front would be a differentiator, not table stakes — nobody else has done it.

## 3. Update-vector defenses (the #1 foundational risk)

This is the load-bearing section given the foundational finding. Findings are more sobering than reassuring: **none of the four players has a cryptographic/provenance answer to this vector — every one relies on process (mandatory re-review or risk-triggered periodic re-review) or on account-hardening (MFA), not on signing or attestation.**

- **Microsoft**: for Teams/Office add-ins, **only manifest/icon/screenshot/version changes trigger mandatory resubmission — backend/web-service logic changes do NOT** [1, official FAQ]. This is a directly documented, real exposure: a compromised or malicious backend push can ship new behavior behind an already-approved manifest without re-review. No equivalent explicit statement was found for Copilot Studio/declarative agents specifically, which is itself notable (the agent validation guidelines are precise about UX/safety requirements but silent on what happens when a publisher changes model/prompt/backend behavior post-approval). No SLSA-style build-provenance or code-signing scheme was found for agent publishing — Entra Agent ID is an identity/authorization construct, not a supply-chain integrity attestation [1, absence-of-evidence]. What Microsoft *has* hardened is the publisher-credential attack surface directly: **Partner Center MFA became mandatory for dashboard sign-in Aug 30, 2025, API testing-capable Sept 30, 2025, and full API enforcement (blocking non-MFA calls) April 1, 2026** [2+, Learn + Tech Community + third-party CSP blogs] — this is a direct, dated response to the "compromised publisher account pushes bad update" vector, the single most concrete update-vector control found across all five research angles.

- **Salesforce**: **no mandatory re-review on ordinary version updates** — a quick attestation in Partner Console suffices, and most updates auto-approve in minutes via a self-review wizard unless they add new objects/callouts/architecture changes [1 official + 2+ trade blogs on the wizard]. Instead, Salesforce runs **risk-triggered periodic re-review** (flagging by time-since-last-review, 6 months–2 years, and magnitude of change), with a 60-day remediation clock on failure and a fee-bearing re-review [1, official]. No package/code-signing mechanism was found anywhere searched — confirmed absence, not just an unturned stone [1, absence-of-evidence across official + trade sources]. MFA is contractually mandatory for all Salesforce logins since 2022 (extending to close an "employee user" exemption June 2026) but this is a general org-security mandate, not a publisher-account-specific control against account-takeover-driven malicious updates [2+, but general-scope, not update-vector-specific].

- **OpenAI**: Apps SDK is the strictest of the four on paper — **any change requires a full new draft version and resubmission for review**; the base MCP server URL is immutable per app (changing it requires a wholly new app, not a version bump) [1, official]. A narrow carve-out exists for "server-only fixes" that "preserve the published contract" (don't change reviewed tool/schema descriptions) — deployable without a new review cycle [1, official]. No evidence was found (either way) of continuous/automated post-approval monitoring for policy drift between formal resubmissions — an open question, not a confirmed gap [1, absence-of-evidence, unconfirmed either direction].

- **Apple/Google reference cases** (the closest real-world analogues to this exact vector): Bitdefender documented **331 Google Play apps, 60M+ downloads, that were clean at initial review and had malicious payloads added only in later updates** specifically to evade first-pass review [2+, Forbes + Bitdefender, March 2025] — this is the single closest real-world precedent for "trusted-then-malicious-update" via the update mechanism itself (as opposed to stolen credentials). Separately, the Dec 2024 Chrome extension campaign and the Dec 2025 Trust Wallet breach both show the *credential-theft* variant of this vector (phished/stolen publish credentials used to push malicious updates to already-trusted, already-installed extensions) [2+ each] — and notably, **no documented Chrome Web Store process change resulted from either incident**, a gap worth naming if the report's argument is that platforms should visibly harden in response to compromise events.

**Bottom line for Sonik:** the foundation's #1 risk has no solved answer among the four current agent-marketplace leaders. The two most concrete, transferable controls found are (a) Microsoft's mandatory-MFA-on-publish-credentials timeline (directly addresses account-takeover-driven malicious updates) and (b) risk-triggered periodic re-review rather than re-reviewing every trivial update (Salesforce's model, which balances review-team capacity against staleness risk). Neither addresses the Bitdefender-style "clean-then-poisoned-update" pattern — that one still requires human/behavioral re-review triggers, which is consistent with the foundation's D3/D7 direction (pair immutable installs with a post-install kill-switch; lean on human review, not automated scanning, since prompt-injection/malicious-update detection is unsolved industry-wide).

## 4. Post-install monitoring / kill-switch models

Capability to actually stop something already installed is uneven and, in several cases, more aspirational than shipped:

- **Microsoft — most mature, most current.** Agent Registry gives admins direct per-agent controls: Install/Uninstall, **Block/Unblock (tenant-wide)**, Delete (permanent, ~24hr propagation), Start/Stop (compute), reassign owner [1, official, very current — updated July 2026]. A separate Power Platform "quarantine" API lets admins isolate a noncompliant agent (visible but non-invocable) during investigation [1, official]. Purview Audit Logs record agent publish/deploy/remove/update actions (180-day retention, 365 with E5) [1, search-summary level, recommend direct confirmation]. **Documented failure mode**: admin block/delete/quarantine actions can silently fail if the agent's environment has IP Firewall in active-enforcement mode, because the request originates from an upstream service IP rather than the admin's own — admins must call the Power Platform API directly to force it [1, official]. Real-time runtime blocking of rogue agent behavior (via Intune+Defender integration) only reaches public preview June 2026 — broader live containment is new, not established [1, roadmap-stage].

- **Salesforce — customer-admin tools exist; Salesforce-side kill-switch does not appear to.** Admins can uninstall packages via standard Setup tools (self-service, not Salesforce-initiated) [1, official]. Agentforce Observability provides live session tracing (inputs, responses, reasoning, guardrail checks) into Data 360 for admin monitoring [2+]. But **no source describes Salesforce's own ability to remotely disable/kill an already-installed malicious package or agent in a customer org** — every control found is either customer-admin-initiated (uninstall) or publisher-initiated (delist, which doesn't touch existing installs) [confirmed gap, absence across official + trade sources]. This is a materially weaker post-install posture than Microsoft's.

- **OpenAI — workspace-level allow/deny only, no granular per-agent control found.** Enterprise/Team/Edu admins get a three-tier setting: allow all third-party GPTs, allow only owner-approved GPTs, or block all [2, help center + secondary write-up] — this is category-level, not a documented mechanism to block one specific named GPT/App while allowing others short of the allowlist model. No continuous post-approval monitoring evidence either way.

- **Apple/Google reference precedent — the strongest kill-switch model overall, at platform scale.** **Google Play Protect actively scans installed apps (350B+ scans/day platform-wide in 2025) and can notify, disable-until-uninstalled, or auto-remove a harmful app from the device** — a genuine remote-disable capability [2+, official Google sources]. Apple's model is certificate-based: revoking an Enterprise cert invalidates every app it signed; Developer ID apps get live OCSP revocation checks at launch; Apple states it can immediately pull an App-Store app and notify affected users [2+, mixed primary/independent]. **Chrome Web Store extensions are notably weaker** — Google cannot remotely disable an installed extension directly; delisting only stops new installs, and Chromium's own maintainers have argued a true remote kill switch is undesirable ("malware can... just flip the kill switch") [1 for the design-rationale quote, 2+ for the general no-remote-disable claim]. This split is instructive: **device-level platforms (iOS/Android) have real kill switches; browser-extension-style distribution does not** — Sonik's installable-capability model is architecturally closer to the extension case (code/config running inside a host app) than to the device-app case, which argues for building the kill-switch deliberately rather than assuming platform-level revocation will cover it.

- **Adjacent/emerging category — AI-agent-specific kill-switch products (2025–2026), useful as a signal of where the market is heading, treat as vendor claims not settled fact.** A widely cited (single-source, needs primary confirmation) industry stat: 60% of enterprises report they cannot terminate a misbehaving AI agent [1]. ServiceNow demoed an "AI Control Tower" kill switch (pause/redirect/stop any agent org-wide, revoke permissions instantly, auto-generate incident) at Knowledge 2026 [1, Fortune]. Microsoft Agent 365 (GA May 1, 2026) frames "Govern" as one of three pillars, explicitly supporting rules-based automatic lifecycle enforcement (expire inactive agents, flag ownerless agents, block risky agents) [2+, best-corroborated of this sub-category]. TrustLogix pitches a cross-platform data-access kill switch plus continuous behavioral-baseline monitoring [1, vendor/press]. The consistent theme across this whole emerging category: **monitoring/alerting investment is outpacing actual stop-mechanism ("kill architecture") investment** industry-wide — the same imbalance the foundation flagged for Sonik (D3) is apparently a market-wide gap right now, not a Sonik-specific deficiency, which is worth stating plainly since it means shipping a real kill-switch would be ahead of the market, not catching up to it.

- **MDM/EMM precedent (mapping model, not a marketplace example).** Microsoft Intune's "Remove apps and configuration" remotely and temporarily strips an app/profile from one managed device (auto-restores in 8–24hrs unless permanently unassigned) [2+, official + independent]. This is a clean structural analogue for "kill one installed capability instance without nuking the whole tenant" — worth using as the mental model for how granular Sonik's kill-switch should be (per-install, not per-org-wide-block, as a floor; tenant-wide block as an escalation).

## 6. Recommendations for Sonik, mapped to gaps, ranked

Mapped against the foundation's D1–D7 gap list (`agent-marketplace-foundational-synthesis-2026-07.md`) plus this lane's own findings. No gate criteria are proposed elsewhere in the corpus, so this ranking is this lane's own read.

1. **[Addresses D3] Build a per-install kill-switch as a first-class primitive now, not later — this is where Sonik can be ahead of the market, not just catching up.** Every player researched has this gap to some degree (Salesforce has none; Microsoft's is newest and has a documented firewall-interaction failure mode; OpenAI's is category-level only). The 60%-of-enterprises-can't-kill-an-agent statistic and the "kill criteria vs. kill architecture" gap suggest most of the industry is *monitoring* without being able to *stop*. A working per-install block/quarantine/delete (modeled on Microsoft's Agent Registry actions or Intune's per-device app removal, not a full tenant-wide nuke) would be a genuine differentiator, and it's cheap relative to building signing/attestation infrastructure.

2. **[Addresses the #1 foundational risk directly] Harden publish-credential security before building anything fancier for the update vector.** No player has solved the update vector cryptographically — the one concrete, dated, transferable control found is Microsoft's mandatory-MFA-on-publisher-account timeline (Aug 2025–Apr 2026 phased enforcement), directly built to stop the "compromised publisher account pushes a malicious update" pattern that both the foundation and this lane's research (Chrome extension phishing campaign, Trust Wallet breach) identify as the actual exploited mechanism — not exotic supply-chain tooling attacks. This is far cheaper than building SLSA-style provenance and addresses the same threat model the foundation flagged as highest-risk.

3. **[Addresses D2 + this lane's trust-tier gap] Make trust-tier a first-class, discovery-visible attribute with published, falsifiable revocation criteria.** All four players gate publishing behind a domain/business-entity-verified account, but strikingly, none publicly discloses concrete revocation/demotion triggers (Salesforce says so explicitly; OpenAI and Google's newest program are simply silent). Publishing clear criteria would put Sonik ahead of, not level with, the field — and pairs naturally with D5 (registered capability-ID namespace), since a trust tier needs a stable identity to attach to.

4. **[Addresses D1 + D7] Use risk-triggered periodic re-review, not blanket re-review-on-every-update, to make human review sustainable at Sonik's scale.** Salesforce's model — most updates auto-approve, but time-since-last-review and change-magnitude trigger a re-review clock with a remediation window — is the most operationally realistic template for a team that (per D7) should lean on human review over automated scanning without drowning that human review team. This directly operationalizes D1's "plain-language install screen" recommendation into an ongoing process rather than a one-time gate.

5. **[Monetization, lowest priority per brief] Start with a free-tier/paid-premium-capability pattern rather than a take-rate model.** Given the commerce marketplace is explicitly later, the Agent.ai/Gumloop freemium pattern (free basic capability, paid premium capability) is the only monetization pattern in this research that doesn't require solving revenue-share mechanics prematurely — defer the 3%-vs-15%-vs-engagement-payout decision until the commerce marketplace work actually starts.

## So-what bullets

- The foundation's single highest-risk finding — a trusted publisher pushing a malicious update — is **unsolved by every major player researched**; none has a cryptographic/provenance answer, only process (re-review triggers) or account-hardening (MFA). Sonik isn't behind the frontier here; the frontier itself is thin, which is an opportunity, not just a risk to match.
- Microsoft's Agent Registry (org-admin pre-publish gate, per-agent block/quarantine/delete, May–July 2026) is the single most transferable model found this lane — it maps almost directly onto "one booking-platform tenant approves one agent capability," which is structurally closer to Sonik's situation than any consumer-scale app store.
- A real per-install kill-switch would put Sonik ahead of the market, not behind it: industry commentary in 2026 explicitly frames most enterprises as having monitoring without a stop mechanism (the "kill criteria vs. kill architecture" gap), and even Salesforce — a mature, 20-year marketplace — has no documented Salesforce-side revocation for already-installed packages.
- No player discloses concrete trust-tier revocation criteria publicly (Salesforce admits this outright; OpenAI and Google are simply silent on their newest verification programs) — publishing Sonik's own criteria clearly would be a differentiator worth calling out, not a compliance checkbox.
- Monetization mechanics in this space are genuinely still settling (OpenAI's Apps SDK discloses no revenue share at all; the OpenAI/Stripe in-chat checkout rollout was reportedly pulled back in parts during 2026) — there's no urgency to lock in a take-rate model now, and the freemium pattern gives Sonik a lower-risk near-term lever while the market matures.
