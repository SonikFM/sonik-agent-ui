MAJOR CONTEXT: Intercom renamed its corporate entity to "Fin" in May 2026, and on June 15, 2026 Salesforce announced a definitive agreement to acquire Fin for ~$3.6B, folding it into Agentforce; deal expected to close Q4 of Salesforce's FY2027, pending regulatory approval. This makes "Fin" now both the product name and (soon) a Salesforce-owned asset — the independent-vendor benchmark story is now an acquisition story.

1. TRACTION / RESOLUTION RATES — Fin has processed 40M+ resolved conversations; Intercom's vendor-reported trailing-30-day resolution rate is ~67% (late 2025); Salesforce's acquisition messaging cites Fin resolving ~76% of support volume autonomously across its customer base. Independent/real-world numbers are notably lower: Intercom's own published case studies put real-world resolution rates at 42-50%, and third-party hallucination testing on Fin's "grounded answer" claims clusters at 38-72% rather than Intercom's advertised <1% hallucination rate. Sources: Intercom Help "Fin AI Agent outcomes" (intercom.com/help/en/articles/8205718), Vhiz AI review 2026 (vhiz.ai/tools/intercom-fin-ai), TechCrunch June 15 2026 (techcrunch.com/2026/06/15/salesforce-acquires-ai-customer-service-platform-fin-for-3-6b), getcor.ai Fin review 2026.

2. REVENUE / CUSTOMER BASE — Fin (the AI product line) surpassed $100M ARR in early 2026, growing ~350%/year, with ~8,000 businesses using it (per SaaSUltra, early 2026). At acquisition time (June 2026), Salesforce/press cite Fin bringing 30,000+ total business customers (this is Intercom's whole customer base, not just Fin-AI users). Parent company Intercom hit $400M total ARR in April 2026 (up from $382M end of 2025; $283.3M in 2024). Intercom raised $250M in venture debt from Hercules Capital in March 2026 to fund AI platform development, shortly before the Salesforce deal. Sources: saasultra.com "Intercom's Fin Is the AI Agent Racing Toward $100 Million in Revenue"; Sacra intercom profile (sacra.com/c/intercom); salesforceben.com "Salesforce Acquires Fin, Adding 30K AI Customers"; Salesforce official press release June 15 2026 (salesforce.com/news/press-releases/2026/06/15/salesforce-signs-definitive-agreement-to-acquire-fin).

3. PRICING — The $0.99-per-resolution outcome-based model has held through mid-2026 with no change announced even after the Salesforce deal (minimum 50 resolutions/month). It has NOT been visibly copied at the exact price point by rivals in these searches, though Decagon uses a hybrid: $50K annual platform fee + per-conversation/resolution pricing (median contract ~$386K/year per third-party data). Customer sentiment on pricing is the single most-cited complaint: cost is unpredictable and scales with success (more resolutions = more spend), and Reddit threads describe bill spikes like $4K/mo → $9K/mo (+120%) after Fin adoption. The core controversy is "assumed resolution" billing — a customer going quiet after Fin's last message counts as a paid resolution even without confirmed satisfaction. Sources: gleap.io "Intercom Fin AI Pricing Explained 2026"; aimdoc.ai "Intercom Fin Pricing: What $0.99 Per Resolution Really Costs"; getcor.ai Fin review 2026; eesel.ai "Decagon vs Sierra: The 2026 guide."

4. CUSTOMER SENTIMENT — Fin holds 4.5/5 across 2,900+ G2 reviews and is G2's #1-ranked AI Agent by review volume. Praise clusters on setup ease ("seamless setup") and answer quality/citation accuracy. Complaints cluster almost entirely on cost predictability and the assumed-resolution billing dispute, not core capability. Some independent hallucination testing suggests the gap between marketed accuracy and measured accuracy is real (see #1). Sources: G2 Fin by Intercom reviews (g2.com/products/fin-by-intercom/reviews); aissist.io "What do Fin AI reviews say?"; getcor.ai Fin review 2026.

5. COMPETITIVE FIELD — Sierra (Bret Taylor + Clay Bavor) has raised $1.585B total, closed a $950M round in May 2026 at $15.8B valuation, hit $150M ARR by Feb 2026, and counts 40%+ of Fortune 50 as customers — positioned as an "Agent OS" for enterprise, managed/done-for-you deployment across voice+chat+SMS+WhatsApp, strongest with Fortune 500 consumer brands. Decagon hit $4.5B valuation Jan 2026, positions as "control over convenience" — CX teams own workflow logic after an engineering-heavy setup; works with Zendesk/Salesforce/Kustomer (not Freshdesk/HubSpot); best fit for >50K conversations/year with engineering resources. Decagon explicitly markets travel/hospitality use cases including restaurant booking upsell scenarios (suggesting tasting menus/private dining during table reservations). Zendesk AI acquired Forethought in March 2026 to bolster its own triage/classification/agent-assist. Salesforce Agentforce requires a Data Cloud purchase (raising TCO) and supports only 17 languages vs. 45-100+ for competitors — and Salesforce is now buying Fin specifically to fix this competitive gap rather than build resolution quality organically. Net positioning: Sierra wins enterprise/Fortune-500-brand-voice deals, Decagon wins engineering-heavy mid-market/tech-company deployments, Fin (via G2 volume + resolution scale) wins SMB/PLG-motion deals, and this whole tier now consolidates partly into Salesforce. Sources: upstartsmedia.com "Decagon, Sierra AI And The Race To Build Customer Support Agents"; Sacra Sierra profile (sacra.com/c/sierra); eesel.ai Decagon vs Sierra 2026 guide; decagon.ai/industry/travel-hospitality; cmswire.com June 2026 Salesforce/Fin coverage.

6. ACTION-TAKING GAP — Fin does have an actions layer: "Fin Tasks" and "Procedures" combined with data connectors let it execute multi-step workflows — canceling orders, processing refunds, updating subscriptions, resetting passwords, scheduling appointments — and Intercom explicitly cites travel/hospitality booking assistance and reservation changes as a use case. Guardrails exist: defined topics Fin must avoid or escalate, and automatic handoff triggers on high-risk content (self-harm, minors, jailbreak attempts, high-risk medical/legal/financial advice). BUT: this is all vendor-documented capability, not independently verified action-reliability data — none of the sources found here contain a rigorous third-party audit of Fin's action success/failure rate specifically (as opposed to conversational resolution rate). The broader industry literature (not Fin-specific) flags that hallucinated actions are qualitatively worse than hallucinated answers because they can be irreversible, and that "summary-only handoff" between AI and human agents loses critical context. Given Fin's own measured/independent resolution and hallucination numbers already run well below vendor claims (42-50% real-world vs 67-76% claimed resolution; 38-72% real-world vs <1% claimed hallucination), it's reasonable to infer the gap between "documented action capability" and "reliable action-taking in the wild" is at least as large, but no sourced number pins that gap for actions specifically. Sources: intercom.com/help/en/articles/9569407-fin-tasks-and-data-connectors-explained; intercom.com/help/en/articles/10539969-best-practices-for-fin-tasks; getcor.ai Fin review 2026 (hallucination-rate gap); fortune.com April 2026 "What do you do when your AI agent hallucinates with your money?" (industry-wide framing, not Fin-specific).

RESEARCHER IMPLICATIONS (what "Intercom-level" means we must match; where the opening is):
- Match the resolution-rate bar realistically, not the marketing number: credibly clear the 42-50% real-world band Fin actually delivers, not the 67-76% advertised — and be prepared to publish our own real numbers since that credibility gap is exactly what erodes trust in this category.
- Match the "grounded, cited answer" UX pattern (source-linked responses) since that's what drives Fin's G2 praise, but don't inherit its hallucination-rate marketing risk — any accuracy claim we make should be independently reproducible.
- Do NOT copy the per-resolution pricing model uncritically — it's Fin's single biggest complaint vector (unpredictable spend, "assumed resolution" billing disputes, 120% bill spikes). A transparent, predictable cost model is a differentiator.
- The action-taking gap is the opening: Fin's actions are vendor-documented but have no independent reliability audit anywhere. Verifiable, audited action-success data (bookings actually committed correctly) where competitors offer only vendor claims = a provable differentiator, not a marketing arms race.
- Consolidation dynamic: market bifurcating into "buy the giant's bundled agent" (Salesforce+Fin) vs "buy the specialist" (Sierra enterprise, Decagon engineering-heavy). A hospitality vertical specialist with provably reliable booking-actions has room — but Decagon already markets restaurant booking upsell, so the use case is contested, not open field.
