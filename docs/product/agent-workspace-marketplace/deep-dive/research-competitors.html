Workflow Competitor Analysis

Composio, n8n, activepieces and Dify all converged on our architecture — workflow-as-data, one generic interpreter, versioned registries. None of the four has a trust boundary. Nobody signs approvals; nobody issues receipts. That absent category is the moat.

The two shaded rows are the finding

Read down the Receipts row: absent · absent · absent · absent, then Sonik. Read the approval-primitive row: an advisory callback the model can satisfy, an HMAC bearer URL, an unguessable URL, an allowlist — no signature, no proof of who approved, anywhere. The strongest approval primitive in the entire set is a bearer-style resume URL or a client-side callback. Host-signed approval, an unrepresentable model-supplied approval, and semantic receipts exist in exactly one column. The architecture rows say the field caught up to workflow-as-data; these two rows say it never crossed the trust boundary.

Composio, head to head

Composio is the named direct competitor: a hosted catalog of ~855 pre-authenticated toolkits, an SDK that is a client to backend.composio.dev, tools as raw server-side JSON Schema, flat TOOLKIT_ACTION slugs. There is no workflow layer at all — multi-step is either event-in/your-code-out triggers or an agent writing imperative code in their Remote Workbench sandbox. No flow document, no state machine, no resume. Their moat is not a workflow engine; it is catalog cold-start plus the meta-tool discovery pattern.

Meta-tool discovery — the thing worth stealing

Instead of dumping hundreds of tool definitions into context, the agent gets ~6 meta-tools and discovers the rest at runtime. Context stays small at 855-toolkit scale. This is the pattern we steal for R3 marketplace discovery and agent-facing capability search — never dump the namespace into context.

Compete or complement

Compete

Head-on on the integration factory and agent tool-availability. Their catalog and discovery UX is the bar — 855 pre-authenticated toolkits and a context-frugal search pattern. We match that surface or we do not enter the conversation.

Complement

Our defensible wedge is governed workflows, host-signed approval and receipts — categories Composio structurally lacks. A Sonik host could even consume Composio as one capability source under our governance layer: their unguarded seam is exactly where our approval boundary sits.

The open seam

Everything Composio does executes in their cloud against their stored credential. The only approval primitive is the experimental Eve provider's client-side, opt-in needsApproval callback; the mainstream OpenAI provider hardcodes require_approval: 'never'. A model-supplied approval is fully representable and fully trusted. Between the agent's decision to execute and the credential-backed API call there is nothing — that gap is where a host-signed approval boundary belongs.

Approval primitives, ranked honestly

Every competitor pauses on a durable primitive and resumes on something the caller possesses or is allowlisted for. None of them signs, and none proves who approved after the fact. Ordered weakest to strongest, the field tops out one rung below a signature.

Cautionary tale — link-borne approval (activepieces ADR 0005)

activepieces waitpoints originally resumed on a bare GET to a resume URL. Email security scanners prefetched those GET links and silently consumed the approvals — the pause was released before any human saw it. The fix was a confirm-page-then-POST so a prefetch cannot complete the action. The lesson generalizes to any link-borne approval: possession of an unguessable ID is not consent, and a GET side effect is reachable by machinery you do not control. This is why we harden the approval endpoint to confirm-then-POST and, above that, gate on a host-signed marker rather than URL possession.

Convergence is the validation; trust is the moat

The four platforms were built by different teams for different markets, and they arrived at the same four architectural patterns. That is not a coincidence to worry about — it is the strongest available evidence that the thesis is correct at 280–855-integration scale. Three of the four patterns we share. The fourth, they all lack.

Ratified read

Compete with Composio on the integration factory — their catalog and discovery UX is the bar. Differentiate on governed workflows, host-signed approval, receipts, and immutable third-party packaging: four categories in which all four competitors are structurally absent, not merely behind. The first three of those four are shipped and full-chain green in packages/tool-contracts/src/workflow-run-state.ts; immutable packaging and the marketplace around it are the planned build. The moat is not a feature they haven't gotten to yet — it is a boundary they have no architecture to cross.
